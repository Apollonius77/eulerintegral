Introduction In Integral Calculus and Ordinary Differential Equations, to reach the solution of a particular problem, it is usual to use the integration by parts method where in some cases, its development can be very extensive, so therefore in this article a general solution will be established for an integral that is the product between a power of degree n and an exponential function of base e. Preliminary To establish the general solution for the integral, the first thing we will have to do is define the derivative of order k of a power of degree n which is \frac{d^kx^n}{{dx}^k}=\frac{n!}{(n-k)!}\ x^{n-k} With n\in\ \mathbb{N},\ k\in\mathbb{N}\ \cup{0}\ and\ k\le n But how we can prove this statement? Well, we are going to take the previous definition and take n values of k For k=0 \frac{d^0}{{dx}^0}=\frac{n!}{(n-0)!}\ x^{n-0}=\frac{n!}{n!}\ x^n=x^n For k=1 \frac{d}{dx}=\frac{n!}{(n-1)!}\ x^{n-1}=\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)...1}{(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)...1}\ x^{n-1}={nx}^{n-1} For k=2 \frac{d^2}{{dx}^2}=\frac{n!}{(n-2)!}\ x^{n-2}=\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)...1}{(n-2)(n-3)(n-4)...1}\ x^{n-2}={n(n-1)x}^{n-2} For k=3 \frac{d^3}{{dx}^3}=\frac{n!}{(n-3)!}\ x^{n-3}=\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)...1}{(n-3)(n-4)(n-5)...1}\ x^{n-3}={n(n-1)(n-2)x}^{n-3} If we continue with this process until k = n . \frac{d^n}{{dx}^n}=\frac{n!}{(n-n)!}\ x^{n-n}=\frac{n!}{0!}x^0=n!\ Which is the nth derivative of the power x^n. With the above, we now move on to prove the following proposition Let\ a\in\mathbb{R}-0\ \ and\ n\in\mathbb{N}\ then \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx\ =\ e^{ax}\sum_{k=0}^{n}{{(-1)}^k\frac{1}{a^{k+1}}}\ \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}\ x^{n-k} Proof We are going to take the integral and apply the integration by parts method \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx Let u = x^n and dv = e^{ax} Then du = nx^{n-1}dx and v = \frac{1}{a}e^{ax} Now we apply the last part of the method which is uv-\int v d u Indeed \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx={\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^n-\int{\ \frac{1}{a}e^{ax}nx^{n-1}dx\ =\ {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^n-\frac{1}{a}n}\int{e^{ax}x^{n-1}}dx\ \mathbf{1}) \ As can be seen on the right side of the equality, we will apply the method again in the next integral, so \int{e^{ax}x^{n-1}}dx u = x^{n-1} and dv = e^{ax} du = {(n-1)x}^{n-2}dx and v = \frac{1}{a}e^{ax} {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^{n-1}-\int{\ \frac{1}{a}e^{ax}{(n-1)x}^{n-2}dx\ =\ {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^{n-1}-\frac{1}{a}(n-1)}\int{e^{ax}x^{n-2}}dx\ Now we put the result above in \mathbf{1}) \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx=\ {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^n-\frac{1}{a}n({\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^{n-1}-\frac{1}{a}(n-1)\int{e^{ax}x^{n-2}}dx\ ) Rearranging some terms \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx=\ {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^n-{\frac{1}{a^2}e^{ax}nx}^{n-1}+\frac{1}{a^2}n(n-1)\int{e^{ax}x^{n-2}}dx\ \mathbf{2}) Then we have for the integral below \int{e^{ax}x^{n-2}}dx u = x^{n-2} and dv = e^{ax} du = {(n-2)x}^{n-3}dx and v = \frac{1}{a}e^{ax} {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^{n-2}-\frac{(n-2)}{a}\int{e^{ax}x^{n-3}}dx\ We put this result in \mathbf{2}) and ordering some terms \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx= {\frac{1}{a}e^{ax}x}^n-{\frac{1}{a^2}e^{ax}nx}^{n-1}+\frac{1}{a^3}e^{ax}n\left(n-1\right)x^{n-2}-\frac{1}{a^3}n\left(n-1\right)\left(n-2\right)\int{e^{ax}x^{n-3}}dx As can be seen, if we repeat the integration by parts, a pattern begins that relates the order of the derivatives and the power of the term 1/a. Indeed And we previously saw that we can define the derivative of x^n in factorial terms, so {\frac{1}{a^{0+1}}e^{ax}\frac{n!}{(n-0)!}\ x}^n-{\frac{1}{a^{1+1}}e^{ax}\frac{n!}{(n-1)!}\ we wanted to demonstrate, which is \int{e^{ax}x^n}dx\ =\ e^{ax}\sum_{k=0}^{n}{{(-1)}^k\frac{1}{a^{k+1}}}\ \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}\ x^{n-k} So that we can understand the above, we will try an example
